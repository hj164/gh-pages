When I was a curator intern, I noticed a lot of racially biased legacy data in the collection database. I created the very first spreadsheet to map out these entries at that institution. Soon, other researchers were using it in their projects.

But it was not enough. There is huge amount of biased data in institutions worldwide, most of them were not open to the public. When I discussed with a professor in my postgrad about how organisations needed a mindset shift to embrace technological change, she said "they are not ready."¬†

The change did come anyway. The following year, I started my PhD when "metaverse" and "AI" became buzzwords. Many industries flocked to new technological solutions without thinking about social impact. These include organisations such as museums, where implementing new technology is often considered ‚Äúchallenging‚Äù. So, I decided to develop a guideline and a workbook to help them formulate long-term strategies. The goal was not to make digital transformation less "challenging" in the future, but to enhance their ability to identify what the transformation entails.

This is what I do: I use research to get people embracing change. I build things to future-proof pathways to change.

I enjoy translating research into catalysts for change. During my PhD, I conducted 2 qualitative studies with cultural organisations in the UK and Australia. The results turned into a report and seminar series on future-oriented digital strategies.

I also brought 2 award-winning culture and education apps to life. I streamlined experimental UX workflows, including co-design with AI agents.

I advocate for digital infrastructure development to be collaborative, beyond organisational or national silos. I lectured at universities, networks, and conferences to help people to discover their oppportunities for collaboration and catalysts for change.¬†

I love to work with people. I love to observe and talk to people to understand how they make meanings with technologies. I enjoy creating solutions for them while working with experts.

Why? Because without knowing how people feel and use them, you will only innovate blindly. Afterall, it's all about how us, humans, can better inhabit in this world.
